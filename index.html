Architecture

We sit between the physical asset and every contractual counterparty — observing the battery, answering to the owner.

§ 01  —  Problem

The data about your battery flows through parties who benefit from how it's read.

Trader

Reports its own revenue

Revenue is easy to attribute. Damage is not. The trader chooses how hard to cycle your asset — and reports on its own performance. Conflict of interest, measured by the same party being measured.

OEM / BMS

Controls the degradation data

State-of-health numbers come from the same vendor who owes you a capacity guarantee. When disputes arise, the evidence lives on their side of the fence.

Consequence

15–20 year obligations priced on their word

LTSAs, warranties, augmentation schedules, financing covenants — all priced on assumptions that nobody on your side can independently verify. Until now.

§ 02  —  What it does

Six capabilities, one source of truth for the asset.

01

Manufacturer benchmarking

Across your portfolio, across vendors
Rank cell suppliers and integrators on real field data across your portfolio: realized throughput, calendar vs. cyclic aging split, thermal behaviour, warranty margin consumption. Feed that evidence into procurement for the next project.
02

LTSA renegotiation data

Long-term service agreements
Service fees, capacity targets, availability definitions, and response-time clauses — priced on assumptions that age badly once the asset is in the ground. We quantify those assumptions against reality and hand you the leverage to reopen terms.
03

Augmentation decision support

When, how much, at what cost
Forward-projected SoH per string and per pack, with confidence intervals. Quantify the revenue uplift of augmenting in year N vs. N+1, against capex and contractual triggers, and stress-test against market price scenarios.
04

Optimiser benchmarking

Revenue-adjusted-for-degradation
Compare optimiser strategies on the metric that actually matters: revenue per cycle-equivalent and per percentage-point of SoH lost. Detect when an optimiser is pushing your asset harder than contracted to inflate its own numbers. Evaluate bids from competing optimisers on like-for-like data before you sign.
05

Warranty enforcement & contractual evidence

Defensible, timestamped, exportable
When delivered revenue underperforms or cells age faster than the warranty curve, you need a clean evidentiary record — not a screenshot from a vendor dashboard. We produce documentation that holds up in contractual disputes and, if it comes to it, legal proceedings.
06

Financing & risk reporting

Lender-ready, periodic
Independent performance reports for lenders and equity partners. We believe a credible, third-party intelligence layer on the asset is a lever on financing terms — early conversations with financiers support this, and we are actively validating it. (Status: thesis under validation.)

How much value is actually at stake — per published benchmarks.

Optimiser underperformance
30–45%
Modo Energy, GB H1 2025: top performers earned ~£100k/MW/yr vs fleet average ~£69k/MW/yr on similar or lower cycling. Spread between operators is structural, not stochastic.
Hidden degradation cost
up to 2×
Yagci et al., J. Energy Storage 2025: stationary 180 Ah LFP cells, capacity fade roughly doubles from 35 °C to 50 °C over 1,500 cycles. Thermal management off-spec compounds silently against the warranty curve.
Warranty margin unused
qualitative
OEM warranty curves are set with commercially motivated margin above physics-bounded degradation. Literature on per-cell degradation modelling (Sarasketa-Zabala, Naumann, Wang et al.) gives the method; the size of the gap is asset-specific and only measurable per-site.
Augmentation mistiming
qualitative
Augmentation timing decisions are priced on forward-SoH assumptions made at contracting. A one-year shift in timing moves capex-equivalent value by material amounts for a 15–20 year merchant asset; the exact figure depends on project economics and market price trajectory.

Optimiser — technical deep-dive

How we measure what an optimiser is actually doing.

Benchmarking an optimiser means comparing its dispatch decisions against counterfactual baselines, decomposing the realised P&L against degradation accrued, and attributing both to specific strategy choices. This page describes the method — the model families we benchmark against, the research we draw on, the metrics we report, and the limitations we own.

§ A  —  What an optimiser actually does

Dispatch under uncertainty, with degradation as a shadow cost.

An optimiser solves a sequential decision problem: given forecasts of prices across multiple markets (DA, ID, FCR, aFRR, mFRR, capacity), forecasts of asset state (SoC, SoH, thermal), and a contractual envelope (cycle limits, depth-of-discharge caps, warranty curve), choose a dispatch trajectory that maximises expected revenue while respecting the envelope. Different optimisers make different choices about which markets to stack, how aggressively to deplete cycle budget, how to handle forecast uncertainty, and how to price degradation as an endogenous cost.

§ E  —  Limitations

What benchmarking cannot tell you.

Honest boundaries of the method: counterfactual baselines are themselves models; degradation attribution has irreducible uncertainty below certain thresholds; market regime changes make historical benchmarks stale; some optimiser strategies are only distinguishable over long time horizons.
